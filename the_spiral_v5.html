Chapter I — The WallFrankfurt, Germany · 1744

THE JUDENGASSE

Begin where it begins. Not with gold. With a wall. A German wall.

The Judengasse of Frankfurt am Main was established in 1462 by order of Emperor Frederick III — a single street in the heart of Germany, three hundred meters long and no wider than four yards at its broadest point, sealed at both ends by heavy gates that locked at nightfall, on Sundays, and on all Christian holy days. Into this fissure in the city's body the entire Jewish population of Frankfurt was compressed. By the eighteenth century, approximately three thousand souls lived in fewer than two hundred houses along that single lane.

The houses could not expand outward — the walls forbade it. So they grew upward, four and five stories into the dark, and backward, carved into ever more impossible subdivisions. Families of twelve in rooms meant for three. Rooftops almost touching overhead. The street at midday a corridor of permanent twilight.

Die Judengasse in Frankfurt · The lane where three thousand people lived behind locked gates
The street at midday a corridor of permanent twilight
Municipal records, Frankfurt Stadtarchiv: Residents were forbidden from the main promenade, parks, farming, most trades, leaving the gate in groups larger than two. Permitted: money-lending, second-hand goods, cattle trading. The restrictions were not incidental. They were architectural.

Into this lane, in 1744, Mayer Amschel was born. The house bore a red shield — rot schild — hung above the door. He was orphaned at twelve. Apprenticed to the banking firm of Oppenheimer in Hannover. And somewhere in the mathematics of deprivation — in the compression of three thousand people into three hundred meters of German stone — something crystallized.

The ghetto did not merely contain the Jews. It produced them. It produced, specifically, their expertise.— Amos Elon, The Pity of It All, 2002

Chapter III — The Day the World Changed HandsBelgium & London, June 1815

WATERLOO

To understand what Nathan Rothschild did on June 20, 1815, you need to understand what the world looked like the day before.

Napoleon Bonaparte had escaped exile on the island of Elba in March and marched back into Paris without firing a shot. The French army, sent to stop him, defected to him instead — regiment by regiment, until the Bourbon king fled and Napoleon was once again Emperor of France. Europe was in panic. The British assembled a coalition army under the Duke of Wellington. The Prussians marshaled under Field Marshal Blücher. The fate of the continent — the political order of Europe for the next century — would be decided on a muddy field in Belgium.

The Battle of Waterloo · June 18, 1815 · William Sadler
"The nearest-run thing you ever saw in your life" — Wellington

The Battle of Waterloo was fought on June 18, 1815. It was a close-run thing — Wellington himself called it "the nearest-run thing you ever saw in your life." Napoleon's Imperial Guard broke against the British lines. Blücher's Prussians arrived on Napoleon's flank. By nightfall, the French army was destroyed.

That was the battle. Now attend to the information.

Nathan Mayer Rothschild · 1777–1836

Nathan Mayer Rothschild, in London, received confirmed intelligence of Wellington's victory via the family's courier network on June 19 or early June 20 — a full day before the British government. The official dispatches did not reach the Cabinet until late on June 21.
